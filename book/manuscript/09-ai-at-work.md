# Chapter 9: AI at Work: Even If You're Not "Techy"

## The Accidental Power User

Derek has worked in operations at a mid-size logistics company for eleven years. He's good at his job. He knows the clients, he knows the routes, he knows which warehouse manager to call when something goes sideways at 4:45 on a Friday.

What Derek is not -- and will happily tell you -- is a "tech person."

He uses Excel because he has to. He types emails with two fingers. When his company rolled out a new project management tool last year, he printed out the instructions and taped them to his monitor. He once described his relationship with technology as "we tolerate each other."

So when Derek's daughter showed him an AI chatbot over Thanksgiving and said "Dad, you should use this at work," he smiled politely and changed the subject.

Three months later, Derek uses AI every single day. Not because he became a tech person. Because he figured out that AI is basically a very fast, very patient coworker who's good at the things he finds tedious.

Here's what happened. One Monday morning, Derek had to decline a meeting invitation from a vendor he wanted to keep a good relationship with. He'd been staring at a blank email for twelve minutes, trying to figure out how to say "no" without burning a bridge. On a whim, he opened the chatbot his daughter had shown him and typed: "Write a professional email declining a meeting invitation from an important vendor. I want to keep the relationship warm and suggest reconnecting next quarter."

Forty-five seconds later, he had a polished, friendly email that struck exactly the right tone. He changed a couple of words, hit send, and sat there for a moment wondering what just happened.

That afternoon, a colleague asked him to pull together some numbers from a spreadsheet. Derek needed a formula to calculate how many days had passed since each date in a column -- something he would normally spend twenty minutes Googling and another twenty minutes getting wrong. He typed the question into the chatbot. It gave him the exact formula. He pasted it in. It worked.

By the end of that week, Derek had used AI to summarize a forty-five-minute meeting from his messy notes, brainstorm ideas for a team-building event, and proofread a report before sending it to the regional director.

He didn't learn to code. He didn't take a course. He didn't watch a single YouTube tutorial. He just learned to ask.

And that's what this chapter is about. AI at work, for people who don't have "tech" anywhere in their job title. No jargon. No setup guides. Just the stuff that will genuinely save you time starting this week.

## Email Drafting and Replies

Let's start here, because this is where most people spend an embarrassing amount of their workday. The average office worker spends over two hours a day on email. A huge chunk of that time isn't reading or deciding -- it's *writing*. Figuring out the right tone. Trying not to sound too blunt, or too passive, or too eager, or too cold. Staring at a cursor.

AI handles this beautifully.

**How to do it:**

1. Open ChatGPT, Claude, or Microsoft Copilot.
2. Tell it what kind of email you need to write and give it context about the situation and tone.
3. Review what it gives you, tweak it to sound like you, and send.

**Example prompts:**

- "Draft a professional response to this email declining a meeting request while keeping the relationship warm: [paste the email you received]"
- "Write a follow-up email to a client who hasn't responded in two weeks. I want to check in without sounding pushy. Keep it under 100 words."
- "I need to ask my manager for a deadline extension on the Henderson project. The reason is that the client changed the scope last week. Make it direct but not defensive."

**Which free tool to use:** ChatGPT, Claude, or Copilot all work well for this. Pick whichever one you already have open.

**The key insight:** You're not outsourcing your communication. You're getting a first draft that takes the hardest part -- staring at the blank page -- off your plate. You still decide what to say. The AI just helps you say it faster.

## Meeting Note Summaries

If you've ever left a one-hour meeting and tried to remember what was actually decided, this one's for you.

The trick is simple: take rough notes during the meeting (or paste in a transcript if your video platform provides one), then let AI organize them into something useful.

**How to do it:**

1. During your meeting, jot down quick notes however you normally would -- bullet points, sentence fragments, shorthand, whatever.
2. After the meeting, paste those notes into Claude or ChatGPT.
3. Ask it to organize them.

**Example prompt:**

"Here are my rough notes from a 1-hour team meeting: [paste notes]. Summarize these into three sections: key decisions that were made, action items with owners and deadlines, and open questions that still need answers."

**Which free tool to use:** Claude handles long, messy text particularly well. Google's NotebookLM is also great for this kind of work. ChatGPT works fine too.

**Why this matters:** Meetings are only useful if people leave them knowing what was decided and who's doing what. AI turns your chicken-scratch notes into a clear summary you can share with the team in two minutes instead of twenty.

## Spreadsheet Formulas and Data Cleanup

This is the one that makes people's eyes light up. If you've ever spent half an hour trying to figure out an Excel formula, only to end up with a cell full of #REF! errors and a burning desire to throw your laptop into the ocean, AI has your back.

**How to do it:**

1. Describe what your data looks like and what you want to accomplish. You don't need to know the formula name or even the right terminology.
2. The AI gives you the formula.
3. You paste it into your spreadsheet.
4. It works.

**Example prompts:**

- "I have a spreadsheet with names in column A and dates in column B. Write me an Excel formula to calculate how many days have passed since each date."
- "I have a list of sales figures in column C. Write a formula that adds up only the values where column D says 'Completed.'"
- "Column A has full names like 'Smith, John'. I need to split these into two columns -- last name and first name. How do I do that in Excel?"

**Which free tool to use:** ChatGPT and Microsoft Copilot are both excellent for spreadsheet formulas. Copilot has the added advantage of working directly inside Excel if your company uses Microsoft 365.

**The important thing to understand:** You don't need to understand *how* the formula works. Seriously. If AI gives you `=DATEDIF(B2,TODAY(),"D")` and you have no idea what DATEDIF means, that's fine. Paste it in, check that the results look right, and move on with your day. You don't need to understand how a car engine works to drive to the grocery store.

## Presentation Outline Creation

Building a presentation from scratch is one of those tasks that feels like it should take thirty minutes but somehow devours an entire afternoon. Most of that time isn't spent on content -- it's spent on *structure*. What order should things go in? How many slides? What's the narrative arc?

AI is excellent at structure.

**How to do it:**

1. Tell the AI what the presentation is about, who the audience is, and what tone you're going for.
2. It gives you a slide-by-slide outline.
3. You fill in the details and build the actual deck.

**Example prompt:**

"Create a 10-slide outline for a presentation about our Q1 sales results. The audience is the executive team. Focus on wins, areas for improvement, and priorities for Q2. Suggest what should go on each slide, including a title and 2-3 bullet points."

**Which free tool to use:** ChatGPT and Claude are both strong here. If you want to go a step further, you can paste the outline into a tool like Gamma or Beautiful.ai, which will generate actual slides from text.

**The result:** Instead of staring at a blank PowerPoint for an hour, you start with a solid framework that you can customize. You're still the one who decides what to emphasize and what to cut. The AI just gets you past the blank-page problem.

## Research and Report Writing

Need to get up to speed on a topic you don't know much about? Need to put together a briefing for your boss? Need to write a summary of industry trends that sounds like you spent a week researching it?

AI won't do the deep, original research for you -- but it's remarkable at synthesizing information and helping you write clearly about topics you're still learning about.

**How to do it:**

1. For research with sources, start with Perplexity -- it searches the web and cites where its information comes from.
2. For writing the actual report, use Claude or ChatGPT to help you organize and polish.

**Example prompts:**

- "Summarize the key trends in the logistics industry for 2026 and organize them into a 1-page executive briefing with section headers."
- "I need to write a 500-word summary of how supply chain disruptions are affecting small businesses. Use a professional but accessible tone."
- "Compare the pros and cons of three project management tools: Asana, Monday.com, and Trello. Format it as a table."

**Which free tool to use:** Perplexity for sourced research (it tells you where its information comes from, so you can verify). Claude or ChatGPT for writing and organizing.

**A critical note:** Always verify facts, especially numbers, dates, and claims about specific companies. AI can sound extremely confident about something that's slightly (or entirely) wrong. The research is a starting point, not a finished product. Your job is to check the important stuff before you put your name on it.

## Proofreading and Editing

You've written the report. You've drafted the email. You've put together the proposal. Now you need a second pair of eyes -- except your coworker is busy, and you're not about to ask your boss to proofread your own work.

AI is a tireless editor that will never judge you for a comma splice.

**How to do it:**

1. Paste your document into the AI.
2. Tell it what kind of editing you want.

**Example prompts:**

- "Proofread this for grammar, clarity, and professional tone. Suggest specific improvements: [paste text]"
- "This email is too long and rambling. Cut it in half while keeping the main points."
- "Review this proposal for any sentences that are confusing or could be misinterpreted by someone outside our department."

**Which free tool to use:** Grammarly is purpose-built for this and catches things in real time as you type. Claude and ChatGPT are better when you want big-picture editing -- reorganizing paragraphs, improving flow, changing tone.

**Why this is valuable:** Everyone has blind spots in their own writing. AI catches the typos, the run-on sentences, and the parts that made perfect sense in your head but don't actually make sense on the page. It's like having a very polite editor available around the clock.

## Brainstorming and Ideation

This might be the single most underrated use of AI at work. When you need ideas -- lots of them, fast -- AI is genuinely hard to beat.

The thing about brainstorming with other humans is that it's slow. People hold back because they're afraid of sounding silly. The loudest voice in the room dominates. Someone derails the conversation with a ten-minute tangent about what they did last summer.

AI doesn't have any of those problems. It just generates ideas. You can ask for ten ideas, reject all of them, and ask for ten more, and it won't be offended.

**How to do it:**

1. Describe the problem or goal clearly.
2. Include constraints (budget, team size, timeline, audience).
3. Ask for a specific number of ideas.

**Example prompts:**

- "We need 10 creative ideas for our company holiday party. Budget is $2,000. Team of 25 people. Mix of introverts and extroverts."
- "Give me 8 ways to improve employee onboarding at a small company with no HR department."
- "I need 5 ideas for a team-building activity that can be done remotely over Zoom in under an hour."

**Which free tool to use:** Honestly, any of them. This is where AI shines. ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot, Gemini -- they're all strong brainstormers. Pick your favorite and start throwing problems at it.

**The trick to getting great ideas:** Don't accept the first batch. Say "those are good, but give me 5 more that are more unconventional" or "I like number 3 -- give me five variations on that idea." Treat it like a brainstorming partner, not a vending machine.

## Let's Talk About the Elephant in the Room

If you've read this far, there's a question that might be nagging at you. Maybe you've thought it yourself. Maybe a coworker has said it out loud. Maybe it's the reason you've hesitated to use these tools at work in the first place.

*Is this cheating?*

Let's address that head-on.

When calculators first appeared in classrooms, people asked whether using them was cheating. When spell-check arrived, people wondered if it was fair to let the computer catch your mistakes. When Google became a verb, some managers worried that employees were "cutting corners" by looking things up instead of memorizing everything.

Nobody has those debates anymore. Calculators, spell-check, and Google are just... tools. Everyone uses them. The question isn't whether you use them -- it's whether you use them well.

AI is the same thing, one step further.

Using AI to draft an email doesn't mean you can't write. It means you'd rather spend three minutes on the email instead of fifteen, so you can use the other twelve minutes on work that actually requires your judgment. Using AI to build a presentation outline doesn't mean you don't understand your material. It means you're skipping the blank-page paralysis and getting straight to the part where your expertise matters.

The quality of your judgment, your relationships, your ability to decide what matters and what doesn't -- that's still entirely you. AI gives you a first draft. You give it direction, context, and the final call. That's not cheating. That's working smart.

---

> **Myth vs. Reality**
>
> **The Myth:** "Using AI at work is cheating."
>
> **The Reality:** It's a tool, like spell-check or a calculator. Nobody calls you a cheater for using Google to look something up or Excel to run a calculation. AI is the next step in that same progression. Your judgment about *what* to ask, *how* to use the output, and *when* the output needs fixing -- that's still entirely yours. The tool does the tedious part. You do the thinking part.

## Try This Now

Here's an exercise you can do in the next three minutes. You don't need to install anything or create an account you don't already have.

**Step 1:** Find your most recent work email draft -- something you've already written, or an email you've been meaning to write but keep putting off.

**Step 2:** Open ChatGPT or Claude (whichever you have access to).

**Step 3:** Paste your draft in and type: "Improve the tone and clarity of this email while keeping it professional and under 150 words."

**Step 4:** Compare the before and after.

That's it. Just notice the difference. Notice what the AI changed -- maybe it tightened a rambling sentence, adjusted the tone, or restructured your points so they land better. Notice what it kept. And notice how long it took.

If the improved version is better, use it. If it's not quite right, tell the AI what to fix. Either way, you just experienced something that's going to save you a lot of time once it becomes habit.

## Key Takeaways

- **You don't need to be "techy" to use AI at work.** If you can describe what you need in plain English, you can use these tools. The skill isn't technical -- it's knowing what to ask for.

- **AI handles the tedious parts so you can focus on the parts that require your brain.** Drafting emails, building outlines, writing formulas, summarizing meetings, proofreading documents -- these are time sinks, not brain work. Let the tool handle them.

- **Using AI at work is not cheating -- it's the same as using any other tool.** Your judgment, your relationships, and your ability to use the output wisely are still what make you valuable. The tool just makes you faster.

- **Start with one use case and build from there.** You don't need to overhaul your entire workflow. Pick the task that annoys you the most -- the weekly report, the email backlog, the spreadsheet that haunts your dreams -- and try AI on that one thing. The rest will follow naturally.

But here's a question worth sitting with for a minute: just because AI *can* do all these things, should it always? What happens when it gets something wrong -- and who's responsible? What about the biases built into these systems, or the things they confidently state that turn out to be completely false?
