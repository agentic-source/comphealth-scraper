# Chapter 6: AI Tools You Can Use Today for Free

## The Best Things in Life Are Free (Including the Most Powerful Technology Ever Built)

Here's something that would have sounded absurd five years ago: the most powerful artificial intelligence tools ever created are available to you, right now, for free.

Not a watered-down demo. Not a 7-day trial with a credit card required. Free. As in, go to a website, make an account, and start using technology that would have cost millions of dollars to access a decade ago.

And yet, most people haven't tried a single one.

It's not their fault. There's a weird assumption floating around that the "real" AI tools are locked behind corporate paywalls or require a computer science degree to operate. People picture enterprise software with intimidating dashboards, or assume that the free versions are so stripped down they're not worth bothering with.

None of that is true.

The free tiers of today's major AI tools are stunningly capable. We're talking about tools that can write, research, analyze documents, create images, help you learn new skills, plan your week, and answer questions that would have taken you hours of Googling to figure out. And they work from your phone, your laptop, your tablet -- whatever you've got handy.

You don't need to spend a dime to start using AI right now. You just need to know where to go and what to try first.

That's what this chapter is for.

## For Writing and Conversations

Let's start with the most common entry point: AI tools that talk to you. These are the chatbots, the assistants, the "ask me anything" tools that most people think of when they hear "AI." They're incredibly versatile, and two stand out as the best places to start.

### ChatGPT -- The All-Rounder

**What it does:** ChatGPT is the tool that kicked off the whole AI conversation. It's a general-purpose AI assistant that can write, brainstorm, explain complex topics, edit your work, generate ideas, help you plan, and about a thousand other things. Think of it as a very knowledgeable coworker who's always available and never judges your questions.

**How to access it:** Go to chat.openai.com and create a free account. There's also a mobile app for iPhone and Android.

**One thing to try first:** Take an email you've been putting off -- maybe one where you need to be diplomatic, or professional, or you just can't find the right words. Paste your rough draft into ChatGPT and type: "Rewrite this email to sound more professional but still friendly." Watch what happens. You'll have a polished version in about three seconds that you can tweak and send. That task that's been sitting in your inbox for two days? Done.

### Claude -- The Thoughtful Analyst

**What it does:** Claude (made by Anthropic) is particularly good at long, careful, nuanced work. If you need something *read* and *understood* -- a contract, a report, a policy document, a long article -- Claude is excellent at digesting large amounts of text and giving you clear, organized answers about it. It's also great at writing that requires a more measured, thoughtful tone.

**How to access it:** Go to claude.ai and create a free account. Mobile apps are available too.

**One thing to try first:** Got a long document you've been meaning to read but keep putting off? Maybe a lease agreement, an insurance policy, or your company's new employee handbook. Upload it to Claude and type: "Summarize the key terms I should pay attention to and flag anything that seems unusual." In under a minute, you'll have a clear breakdown of a document that would have taken you an hour to slog through on your own.

## For Research and Learning

Sometimes you don't need a writing partner -- you need answers. Good answers, with sources, not just opinions. These next two tools are built for exactly that.

### Google Gemini -- The Ecosystem Player

**What it does:** Gemini is Google's AI assistant, and its superpower is how well it integrates with everything Google. If you already live in the Google world -- Gmail, Google Docs, Google Search -- Gemini fits right into that flow. It's strong at explanations, creative tasks, and connecting dots across topics.

**How to access it:** Go to gemini.google.com and sign in with your Google account. You probably already have one.

**One thing to try first:** Pick something you've always been curious about but never quite understood. Type: "Explain quantum computing to me like I'm someone who loves cooking -- use only kitchen analogies." This does two things. First, you'll actually understand quantum computing (or whatever you choose). Second, you'll see how AI can tailor explanations to *your* brain, not some generic textbook version. Try it with anything: economics, astronomy, how your car engine works. Make the analogy personal to something you already know well.

### Perplexity -- The Research Assistant That Shows Its Work

**What it does:** Perplexity is different from the others in an important way: it's built specifically for research, and it *cites its sources*. When you ask it a question, it doesn't just give you an answer -- it tells you *where* that answer came from, with links. If you've ever wished Google would just *answer your question* instead of giving you ten blue links to click through, Perplexity is what you've been waiting for.

**How to access it:** Go to perplexity.ai and create a free account.

**One thing to try first:** Think of a decision you're trying to make where you need actual facts, not just opinions. Maybe you're researching heat pumps vs. traditional HVAC systems, or trying to figure out if a Roth IRA conversion makes sense this year, or comparing school districts in two neighborhoods. Type your question into Perplexity and watch it pull together a sourced, organized answer in seconds. No more opening 15 browser tabs and trying to piece it together yourself.

## For Work and Productivity

These tools aren't just for curiosity -- they can make your actual workday shorter and less painful. If you deal with meetings, documents, or any kind of written work (so, basically everyone), pay attention here.

### Microsoft Copilot -- Already on Your Computer

**What it does:** Copilot is Microsoft's AI assistant, and if you use Windows or the Edge browser, it's already sitting there waiting for you. It can summarize documents, help you write, answer questions, and pull together information from different sources. If your workplace runs on Microsoft products, this one is particularly useful because it speaks that language natively.

**How to access it:** Go to copilot.microsoft.com, or just look for the Copilot icon in Windows or Edge. Sign in with your Microsoft account.

**One thing to try first:** After your next meeting (or any meeting you have notes from), paste the transcript or your notes into Copilot and type: "Summarize this meeting and pull out the action items with who's responsible for each one." Instead of spending 20 minutes writing up meeting notes nobody will read, you'll have a clean summary in seconds. Send it to the team. Look like the most organized person in the room.

### Google NotebookLM -- Your Personal Research Analyst

**What it does:** NotebookLM is one of the most underrated free AI tools available. Here's the concept: you upload documents -- PDFs, Google Docs, web links, whatever -- and then you can have an AI conversation specifically about *those documents*. It only draws from what you gave it, which means it stays focused and doesn't make things up from random internet knowledge.

**How to access it:** Go to notebooklm.google.com and sign in with your Google account.

**One thing to try first:** Upload something meaty -- a long report from work, a textbook chapter, a stack of research papers, or even the manual for that appliance you never read. Then start asking questions. "What are the main arguments in this document?" "What does section 4 say about liability?" "Summarize this in language a non-expert would understand." It's like having a research assistant who actually read all the material you didn't have time for.

## For Creative and Visual Work

AI isn't just about text. If you ever need to create something visual -- social media posts, presentations, flyers, or even just a quick graphic -- these tools can help, and you don't need to be a designer.

### Canva AI -- Design Without Being a Designer

**What it does:** Canva has been the go-to tool for non-designers for years, and now it's packed with AI features. Magic Write generates text for social media posts, presentations, and marketing copy. Magic Design creates layouts based on your content. And the AI image tools let you generate and edit visuals without touching Photoshop.

**How to access it:** Go to canva.com and create a free account. Many of the AI features are available on the free tier.

**One thing to try first:** Next time you need to post something on social media -- for yourself or a small business -- open Canva and try Magic Write. Give it a topic and a tone ("Write a casual Instagram caption about our weekend sale on handmade candles"), and you'll have several options in seconds. Pick one, drop it into a Canva template, and you've just created a professional-looking social post in about two minutes. No copywriter needed.

### Grammarly -- The Writing Coach in Your Browser

**What it does:** Grammarly has evolved well beyond spell-check. Its AI now helps with tone, clarity, conciseness, and even complete rewrites of sentences or paragraphs. It works everywhere you write -- email, documents, social media, messaging apps -- because it lives right in your browser.

**How to access it:** Go to grammarly.com and install the free browser extension. It works in Chrome, Firefox, Edge, and Safari.

**One thing to try first:** Install the extension and then just go about your day. The next time you write an email, Grammarly will quietly start suggesting improvements. Not just typos -- it'll flag sentences that are unclear, suggest more concise phrasing, and tell you if your tone sounds too harsh or too passive. It's like having an editor looking over your shoulder, but one who never makes you feel bad about a misplaced comma.

## A Note About What "Free" Means

Let's be honest about something. Every tool on this list has a paid tier. ChatGPT has Plus, Claude has Pro, Perplexity has Pro, and so on. The companies behind these tools are businesses, and the free tiers exist partly to get you hooked so you'll upgrade.

But here's the thing: the free versions are genuinely, remarkably useful. They're not crippled demos. For most people, most of the time, the free tier does everything you need. You might hit some limits -- a cap on how many questions you can ask per day, or some advanced features that are locked -- but for getting started and handling everyday tasks, free gets you very far.

All of these tools have free tiers as of 2026. That could change, of course. This space moves fast. Features that required a paid subscription last year might be free now, and vice versa. But the trend has generally been toward *more* free access, not less, because these companies are competing hard for your attention.

You also don't need to use all of them. That's important. Don't look at this list and think, "Great, eight new accounts to manage." Pick one or two that fit your life. If you write a lot of emails, start with ChatGPT or Claude. If you do a lot of research, try Perplexity. If you're a visual person, check out Canva. You can always explore more later.

- **The most powerful AI tools are free to use right now.** ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Perplexity, Copilot, NotebookLM, Canva AI, and Grammarly all offer free tiers that are genuinely useful -- not stripped-down demos.
